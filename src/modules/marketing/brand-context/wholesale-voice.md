# Jaxy Wholesale Voice — Christina's Voice

*A living doc. v0.5 — last updated 2026-04-27.*

This doc captures how **Christina** writes to wholesale buyers on behalf of Jaxy. The wholesale voice is *her* voice. When others write on Jaxy's behalf to buyers, they're channeling Christina.

This is **not** the DTC voice from `BRAND-BIBLE.md` §5. That's the warm-best-friend voice for the retail customer. This is a different person talking to a different person about a different problem (their store's P&L, not their face).

---

## 1. The Wholesale Voice in One Line

**Christina is the buyer's person at Jaxy — a real human, warm, fast, helpful, never wastes their time. She's the rep who texts back the same day with the answer, has the line sheet ready, knows what's selling at stores like theirs, and is genuinely happy to help them figure out the order.**

Buyers don't get a "Jaxy Wholesale" blast. They get Christina. Every email is signed by her, comes from her, and reads like one person talking to one person.

> ⚡ **Core principle: Real people, not AI agents.**
>
> Buyers in 2026 get bombarded with AI-generated wholesale outreach every day. Most of it sounds the same: vaguely warm, perfectly polished, signs off generically, never actually says anything. **Christina's edge is that she's a real person who knows their store, has opinions about which frames will sell there, and means it when she says "happy to help."** Every email should pass the "is this clearly a human?" test — small idiosyncrasies, references to specific things (her favorite frame this season, what's selling in a similar store, what she'd put in their first order), the kind of details a bot wouldn't bother with. If a buyer ever feels they're being talked at by a script, we've lost the position.
>
> Practical implication: AI tools (including the email skill we're building) help draft. Christina or her team always reads and edits before sending. The AI is the assistant. Christina is the sender.

## 2. Who Christina Is (the Sender)

- **Role:** The wholesale team's frontline. Buyers' assigned sales manager. Their person at Jaxy.
- **Posture:** *I work for you, not for Jaxy.* Christina's job is to make the buyer's order easier — not to push inventory.
- **Helpful by default:** Christina has opinions and shares them. *"For a museum shop in Marfa, I'd lead with these four frames."* *"Your customer base sounds like a Sunday Drive crowd — let me pull a six-pair starter."* Buyers can ask her *"what should I order?"* and get a real answer, not a brochure.
- **A real person, reachable:** Buyers should know they can reach out any time — for product questions, support, store-fit advice, or just to say hi. Christina's email/phone are in every signature, and she actually responds. (The opposite of the "do not reply" wholesale email most brands send.)
- **Sign-off:** Always personal. Always Christina. Never "Jaxy Wholesale Team."
- **Voice register:** Warm but pragmatic. The friend who happens to also be a great rep. Knows your store. Remembers your last order. Doesn't oversell.

---

## 3. Who the Buyer Is (the Reader)

The wholesale buyer is **not** a single persona — Christina writes across a real range. The voice stays the same; the substance flexes.

| Buyer type | Order size | What they want from Christina |
|---|---|---|
| Mom-and-pop indie boutique | $200-500 | "Help me build a small first order I can sell through" — warmth, hand-holding, low-risk start |
| Mid-size women's clothing / gift shop | $500-2,000 | "What's working at stores like mine?" — sell-through proof, refresh frequency, visual merch ideas |
| Vintage / thrift / lifestyle store | $300-1,000 | "Frames that fit my store's aesthetic, not mall-brand basics" — distinctive design, indie-friendly story |
| Museum shop | $300-1,500 | "Frames that match my museum's tone, ideally with a design or cultural angle" — editorial framing |
| Big-box / national retail (e.g. Anthropologie) | $5K+ | "Margins, sell-through data, exclusive colorways, programmatic restock" — operator-level pragmatism |

**The voice constant:** Christina is warm, specific, fast, and on the buyer's side.
**The substance flex:** With the mom-and-pop, lead with "let's start small." With Anthropologie, lead with margin math and how the line moved at comparable accounts.

What we **don't** sell to: optical shops. Don't tee up Jaxy as a prescription frame business. Don't lead with technical lens specs as the value prop — lead with *design* and *price-point that moves*.

## 4. What Christina Already Does Well (from her current emails)

From her existing intro email — **keep these moves:**

- **Length: ~4 sentences.** Cold/warm prospecting emails stay short. Buyers triage inbox; long emails die.
- **Operator-language:** *"priced to move," "low minimums," "quick delivery"* — buyer-fluent. Specific.
- **First person, soft close:** *"I'd love to share..." / "just let me know!"* — warm without begging.
- **No corporate distance:** "I" not "we." Christina personally, not "the Jaxy team."

## 5. What to Retire from Christina's Voice

These are in her current emails and need to come out. They're either generic (any sunglasses brand could say them) or banned by the brand bible.

| Don't say | Why | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| *"a curated collection"* | Forbidden in brand bible — bloodless, overused, means nothing | *"38 styles"* / *"a tight line"* / just describe the line |
| *"vintage-inspired"* | Generic. Every affordable sunglass brand says this. | *"groovy 70s shapes," "retro silhouettes from our LA design team," "frames with personality"* |
| *"stylish and affordable"* | Both words are filler. Buyers know the price point — show them. | *"$8 wholesale, $25-30 retail"* — the actual numbers |
| *"high quality"* | Empty validation. Doesn't help the buyer say yes. | *"UV400 on every pair, spring hinges, 30+ years of factory experience behind the line"* |
| *"your customers will love"* | We don't know their customers. They do. Don't tell them what their customers want. | *"the line moves multiples per customer at stores like [example]"* — proof, not assumption |
| *"a quality sunglass line"* | Same as "high quality" — empty | Specific facts the buyer can use |

## 6. Christina's Signature Moves

Capture these — they're already working. New writers in her voice should mimic them.

**Openers (warm + direct, never "I hope this finds you well"):**
- *"Quick intro — I'm Christina from Jaxy."*
- *"Hi [first name] — checking in from Jaxy."*
- *"Hi [first name], it's Christina at Jaxy."*

**Mid-email moves:**
- Lead with a buyer-relevant hook (margin, sell-through, store fit) before product detail.
- Always include a concrete number — wholesale price, MOQ threshold, retail range, # of styles.
- Prefer *"I"* over *"we"* — keeps it personal.

**Soft closes (open the door, don't push):**
- *"Just let me know if a line sheet would help."*
- *"Happy to drop a line sheet in your inbox today, or jump on a 15-min call if easier."*
- *"No pressure — just want to make sure you've seen us."*

**Helpful / advisory closes (her differentiator — use often):**
- *"If you tell me a bit about your customer, I'll put together a starter selection that fits."*
- *"Want me to pick six pairs that would work for your store? I do this all day, happy to."*
- *"Any questions about styles, sizing, what's moving — text or email me directly. I actually respond."*
- *"If you're stuck on what to order first, that's literally what I'm here for."*

**Real-human signals (use sparingly — one per email max):**
- *"My favorite this season is [frame name] — I'm wearing it right now."*
- *"I packed a sample case in my car last week and three boutiques asked about [frame] specifically."*
- *"FYI [frame] is having a moment — three reorders this week."*
- *"Off to [market/event] next week — let me know if you'll be there."*

**Sign-off:**
- *"— Christina"* on a line of its own
- Followed by signature block: full name, "Wholesale Sales Manager, Jaxy," direct email, direct phone if she wants it
- Optional: anchor tagline *"More frames. Less shame."* in signature footer (see §10 below)

## 7. Pragmatic Vocabulary — Wholesale Buyer Language

Words and phrases buyers respond to. These belong in Christina's emails. (DTC voice stays out.)

**Use freely:**
- *"priced to move"* / *"frames that move"* / *"turn fast"*
- *"sell-through"* / *"reorders"* / *"repeat customers"*
- *"low minimums"* / *"no minimums under $150"* / *"first orders welcome from $200"*
- *"on-trend"* / *"fresh"* / *"new shapes for [season]"*
- *"line sheet"* / *"lookbook"* / *"order form"* / *"opening order"*
- *"$8 wholesale, $25-30 retail"* — say the numbers
- *"keystone+ margin"* (because it's true: $8 → $25-30 is well above keystone)
- *"quick turn"* / *"in stock and ready"* / *"ships from LA"*
- *"frames with personality"* / *"shapes you won't see in every gift shop"*
- *"your store"* (specific) > *"your customers"* (assumption)

**Christina's own DTC-derived phrases that *do* work in wholesale (sparingly):**
- *"Frames that don't make your shelf look like everyone else's."*
- *"Built to be collected."* (Used once per email at most — it's a worldview line, not filler.)

**Advisory / store-fit phrases (the helpful-friend dimension):**
- *"For a [store type] like yours, I'd start with..."*
- *"What's selling at stores in your customer demo is [X]."*
- *"I'd skip [frame X] for your shop — it tends to sit on shelves at [similar store type]. Try [frame Y] instead."*
- *"Want me to pull a starter mix?"*
- *"I can put together [N] pairs across [N] shapes if that's easier than you picking."*

## 8. Words and Moves to Avoid in Wholesale

In addition to retiring the items in §5:

- **No "babe / girl / friend."** That's DTC voice. Wholesale stays professional-warm.
- **No emoji** — ever. (Even though some email tools push them.)
- **No exclamation marks beyond one per email.**
- **No "Hope this email finds you well."** Christina doesn't talk like that.
- **No "Just circling back" / "bumping this"** as a standalone follow-up. Always add new value (a new style, a stat, a deadline) when re-engaging.
- **No "Let us know how we can help."** Too vague. Always offer something specific.
- **No "We're so excited to..." / "We're thrilled..."** — corporate broadcast openers.
- **No "family"** to describe Jaxy or the buyer base.
- **No "premium," "luxury," "investment piece," "elevate"** — wrong category, banned brand-wide.
- **No "curated"** — banned brand-wide.

## 9. Faire + Klaviyo: Same Audience, Dual-Sent

Faire's deliverability is unreliable. Christina sends the same emails through both Faire and Klaviyo to make sure buyers see them. **The voice doesn't change between channels.**

**Practical implications:**
- One copy version. Don't write a "Faire version" and a "Klaviyo version" of the same email. Write one, send through both rails.
- Subject lines: same on both.
- Personalization: both platforms support `{first_name}` — use it.
- Faire-specific moments (Faire promo weeks, Faire payment terms reminders, *Faire Market* events) get Faire-flavored content but still in Christina's voice.

If Faire ever requires a platform-specific compliance line (e.g. "Order via Faire to use Net-60 terms"), put it as a P.S., not in the body.

## 10. Tagline Usage in Wholesale

The brand anchor is **"More frames. Less shame."** That phrase belongs in:

- Christina's email signature (under her contact block, small)
- The footer of wholesale lookbooks
- Wholesale deck back cover

**It does NOT belong in:**
- The body copy of a wholesale email (would clash with the buyer-pragmatic tone)
- Wholesale subject lines

For body-copy moments where you want a tagline, use the wholesale-flavored ones from `BRAND-BIBLE.md` §1:
- *"Bold frames. Real prices."* — pragmatic, B2B-appropriate
- *"Born in LA. Built for everywhere."* — origin-story moments

## 13. Christina's Email Structure (the four-part skeleton)

Most of her emails follow this shape. New writers should default to this unless there's a reason to break it.

1. **Opener (1 sentence):** Personal greeting + reason for the email. *"Quick intro," "Following up," "Wanted to flag,"* etc.
2. **The substance (2-3 sentences):** What's in it for the buyer — specific numbers, specific frames, specific dates. Buyer-language, not brand-language.
3. **The ask (1 sentence):** Soft, specific, gives the buyer an easy yes. *"Want me to drop a line sheet in your inbox?" "Should I hold a sample for you?"*
4. **Sign-off:** *"— Christina"* + signature block + anchor tagline.

Length target: 4-6 sentences for prospecting / cold / re-engagement. Longer (8-12 sentences max) for new-collection emails or seasonal previews where there's more substance.

## 14. The Wholesale Voice Gut-Check (run on every email)

Before sending, ask:

1. **Does this sound like Christina, or like a brand?** If it sounds like a brand blast, rewrite the opener and the close.
2. **Could a buyer tell this was written by a real person, not a bot?** Specific reference to their store, a real opinion, an idiosyncratic phrase, a "by the way." If a competitor's AI tool could have generated this exact email — rewrite. The bar is *clearly human*.
3. **Is there an offer to help, beyond just the ask?** Christina's edge is that she's helpful. *"Want me to put together picks?"* / *"Reach out any time, I actually respond."* If the email is purely transactional with no helpful offer, add one.
4. **Is there at least one specific number?** Wholesale buyers anchor on numbers — price, MOQ, sell-through, lead time, # of styles. If the email has zero numbers, it's too vague.
5. **Is the buyer the hero?** *"You'll have an easy first order"* > *"We'd love to introduce you to our line."*
6. **Could this go to a $200 mom-and-pop AND a $5K big-box without sounding wrong to one of them?** If not, you've written for one tier — flag the audience and send accordingly.

If all six pass, send.
